Established in 1806 in Kolkata, the State Bank of India (SBI Bank) underwent significant transformations in its early years. Within three years of its inception, it acquired its charter and was rebranded as the Bank of Bengal in 1809. Notably, it was India's inaugural joint-stock bank, sponsored by the Bengal Government. Alongside the Bank of Bengal, the Bank of Madras and the Bank of Bombay were also integral parts of this joint venture, playing pivotal roles in modernizing banking practices. These three banks emerged as Anglo-Indian institutions, driven by several factors, including the stagnant state of the Indian economy, the demands of local European commerce, and the financial imperatives of imperial governance. The evolution of the State Bank of India was influenced by ideas imported from England and Europe, as well as shifts in the local trading landscape and India's economic ties with Europe and the global economy.



The State Bank of India (SBI) stands as a formidable entity within the banking landscape, bolstered by several key factors. It holds the distinction of being the oldest bank in the country based on the size of its balance sheet. Moreover, its substantial market capitalization, extensive network of bank branches, and robust profit margins position it as a formidable competitor against private sector banks nationwide. Currently, the bank is diversifying its business portfolio through strategic partnerships, presenting significant growth opportunities. These partnerships encompass various sectors, including General Insurance, Pension Funds, Private Equity, Custodial Services, Mobile Banking, Structured Products, Advisory Services, and Point of Sale Merchant Acquisition. Furthermore, SBI is focusing on expanding its wholesale banking capabilities to cater to the high-end corporate sector, offering a plethora of services and products. The bank is also venturing into derivative instruments and structured products, while simultaneously consolidating its global treasury operations. Presently, SBI holds the distinction of being the largest arranger of external commercial borrowings in the country and a major provider of infrastructure debt. Additionally, it stands as the sole Indian bank listed in the Fortune 500 roster. In addition to its core banking services, the State Bank of India has been actively engaged in non-profit initiatives since 1973, exemplified by its Community Services Banking program. Through this initiative, administrative offices and branches across the nation actively support and participate in numerous social welfare activities and causes. Furthermore, the bank has introduced three digital banking platforms to streamline financial transactions for its customers. Two of these platforms are tailored to provide doorstep banking services, employing TAB banking methodology. One caters to housing loan applicants, while the other serves customers seeking to open savings accounts.
